A working library and session templates — built around descents, vert, and a left chain that needs respect

HOW TO USE THIS BOOK

A finish-focused mountain ultra is won by legs that still brake on the descents at hour 18 and a body that stays upright and loaded for 24 hours. This strength work serves that — not aesthetics, not max numbers.

The plan's Str. column names a template (T1–T5) for each week; the full prescriptions live here. Every exercise in the templates is specified in the library below.

READ THIS FIRST — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE

Alex Effer's prescribed corrective work is the backbone of the lower-leg and left-chain training. This book is a general strength supplement that sits around his rehab, never over the top of it. Where exercises here touch the calf or the left kinetic chain (foot → tibia → hip → knee → calf), his guidance wins every time. Run any new calf loading past him before adding volume — the working picture is plantarflexor hyperexcitability / EAMC, not a tissue you simply load harder.

THE PRINCIPLES

  • One quality session per week, ~45–55 min including warm-up, on the erg/rowing day. Rowing is general aerobic and posterior-biased, so strength rounds out what the erg misses: single-leg control, eccentric braking, lateral chain, trunk endurance.
  • Rotate the emphasis week to week (Templates 1 → 2 → 3 → repeat). Because it's only once weekly, each session is full-body with a different lean, not a body-part split.
  • Eccentric / descent-protective work is the priority bucket. If a session gets cut short, keep those movements.
  • Quality over load. Leave 2–3 reps in reserve (RIR). This builds durable tissue and control, not a 1RM. Sore legs must not compromise the Friday Peak day.
  • Periodise down into the race. Heaviest in base (Jun–Aug), trimmed through build, maintenance-only from ~mid-Oct so it never blunts the peak mountain weeks.
  • Never stack heavy lower-body the day before a long Peak day. Keep strength early-week.

READING THE PRESCRIPTIONS

NOTATION MEANING
3 × 8 / leg 3 sets of 8 repetitions on each leg
Tempo 3-1-1-0 Seconds per phase: lowering (eccentric) – pause at bottom – lifting – pause at top. A high first number = slow, controlled lowering — the descent-protective stimulus.
RIR 2–3 Reps In Reserve — stop with 2–3 good reps left in the tank. Never grind to failure.
Iso :45 Isometric hold for 45 seconds

WARM-UP — EVERY SESSION, ~6–8 MIN

  • 2–3 min easy on the erg you just finished, or a brisk walk, to stay warm.
  • Effer's activation/breathing drills if prescribed for that day — these come first.
  • Glute activation: banded clamshells + glute bridges, ~15 reps each, to set the glute → hamstring → calf firing order before loading.
  • 5–8 bodyweight reps of the session's first main lift as a primer set.

A · DESCENT-PROTECTIVE & ECCENTRIC

Priority bucket

The priority bucket — loaded, slow lowering is the closest gym stimulus to braking downhill. If a session gets cut short, these movements survive the cut. Doses are base-phase starting points; progress per the rules at the back.

A1Eccentric split squat

PrioritySingle-leg

The single best gym mimic of descending: loaded knee flexion under a slow, controlled lower. Builds the quads' braking capacity so they survive 6,280m of downhill.

Tempo
4-1-1-0
Dose
3 × 6–8 / leg
Progress
Hold dumbbells → slower eccentric
Setup
Split stance, rear foot on the floor (or lightly on a low bench). Torso tall, weight through the front mid-foot.
Execution
Lower slowly and under control, front knee tracking over toes, until the front thigh is near parallel. Drive up at normal speed. The lowering is the whole point.
Watch
Keep the knee stable, no inward collapse — that's the left-chain pattern showing up. Stop the set if control goes.

A2Step-down (forward & lateral)

PrioritySingle-leg

Directly trains the controlled, single-leg lowering of a downhill foot-plant, plus the hip/knee control that stops the left knee hyperextending and the foot over-pronating.

Tempo
3-1-1-0
Dose
3 × 8 / leg
Progress
Higher box → light DB
Setup
Stand on a step/box (start ~15 cm, build height). One leg working, other leg hanging off the edge.
Execution
Slowly lower the free heel toward the floor by bending the standing leg — tap lightly, don't dump weight onto it — then control back up. Lateral version: step off to the side to load the hip abductors / lateral chain.
Watch
Hips level (no drop on the free side), knee over mid-foot. This is a control drill before it's a strength drill.

A3Spanish squat (isometric)

Priority

Knee-friendly quad loading with low joint stress — builds tendon-tolerant quad strength without the impact, useful when legs are already loaded from hill days.

Hold
Iso :30–:45
Dose
4–5 holds
Progress
Add held weight / longer hold
Setup
Loop a stout band around a fixed post and behind both knees. Lean back into the band, feet planted.
Execution
Sit back into a squat (~60–90° knee bend) with shins vertical and hold. Quads do the work; the band takes the shear.

A4Reverse Nordic

Priority

Loaded eccentric lengthening of the quads through a big range — potent protection against late-race downhill quad blow-up. Advanced; ease in.

Tempo
4-0-2-0
Dose
2–3 × 5–8
Progress
Greater lean angle
Setup
Kneel tall on a pad, knees hip-width, torso and hips in one line.
Execution
Keeping a straight line from knee to head, lean back slowly under control as far as you can hold, then return. Small range first; build over weeks.
Watch
No hinging at the hip (don't fold) and no lower-back arch. Quads only. Start tiny — this gets sore.

A5Eccentric heel drop

Calf · check Effer

Classic calf/Achilles eccentric loading. Relevant to descending and push-off — but this is exactly the tissue under the working diagnosis.

EFFER GOVERNS THIS ONE

Do not add eccentric calf loading on your own initiative. Use only the heel-drop volume, range and frequency Effer has set, and the cues he's given (heel elevation, big-toe wedge, breathing). The notes below are descriptive, not a prescription.

Execution (if cleared)
Rise on both feet, shift to the working leg, lower the heel slowly below a step. Soleus bias: same with the knee bent.

B · SINGLE-LEG STRENGTH & STABILITY

Single-leg

Mountain running is one leg at a time on uneven ground — these build the unilateral strength and balance that bilateral squats miss.

B1Bulgarian split squat

Single-leg

The workhorse single-leg strength builder.

Tempo
3-0-1-0
Dose
3 × 8–10 / leg
Progress
DBs → goblet → slow tempo
Setup
Rear foot elevated on a bench, front foot far enough forward that the knee stays over mid-foot at the bottom.
Execution
Lower under control until the front thigh is ~parallel, drive through the front heel/mid-foot to stand. Keep the torso tall-ish.
Watch left side
Hip stays level, knee doesn't dive in, no rotation. Quality over load on the weaker chain.

B2High step-up

Single-leg

The closest gym mirror of power-hiking a steep climb — the GTCB's bread and butter. Trains hip/knee extension from a deep, loaded position.

Tempo
2-0-2-0
Dose
3 × 8 / leg
Progress
Hold DBs / loaded pack
Setup
Box at mid-thigh height (knee bent ~90° when the foot's on it).
Execution
Drive up through the top leg with minimal push-off from the floor leg — make the working leg do it. Control the lower back down (don't just drop).
Note
Loading with the race pack ties it straight to power-hiking specificity.

B3Single-leg Romanian deadlift

Single-legPosterior

Builds the glute/hamstring chain unilaterally and trains balance + hip control on one leg — exactly the stability needed on technical, fatigued descents.

Tempo
3-0-1-0
Dose
3 × 8 / leg
Progress
Heavier DB / KB
Setup
Stand on one leg, soft knee, weight in the opposite hand (or both).
Execution
Hinge at the hip, back flat, free leg extending behind as a counterweight; lower until the hamstring loads, return by squeezing the glute.
Watch
Hips square to the floor (resist the open-up rotation) — this directly trains against the left-chain pattern.

C · POSTERIOR CHAIN & GLUTES

Posterior

Strong hamstrings and glutes power the climbs, protect the knees on descents, and take load off the over-eager calf.

C1Romanian deadlift (bilateral)

Posterior

The hip hinge also reinforces the posture you'll hold for 24 hours.

Tempo
3-0-1-0
Dose
3 × 8–10
Progress
Add load, keep form
Execution
Barbell or DBs; hinge at the hips with a flat back, bar close to the legs, lower to mid-shin or where the back stays neutral, drive hips forward to stand.

C2Hip thrust / glute bridge

Posterior

Direct glute strength — the prime mover for climbing and the muscle that should fire first in the chain. Strong glutes take load off the over-eager calf.

Tempo
2-1-1-0
Dose
3 × 10–12
Progress
Load → single-leg
Execution
Upper back on a bench (thrust) or floor (bridge), barbell/DB across the hips, drive through heels to full hip extension, squeeze glutes hard at the top, lower with control.

C3Banded lateral walk / clamshell

PosteriorLateral chain

Targets glute medius — the hip stabiliser that keeps the pelvis level and stops the left-side collapse pattern on every single foot-plant over 102km.

Dose (walk)
3 × 10 steps / way
Dose (clam)
3 × 12–15 / side
Progress
Stronger band
Execution
Band above knees or ankles. Lateral walk: half-squat, step sideways keeping tension, hips level. Clamshell: side-lying, knees bent, open the top knee without rolling the pelvis back.
Note
Doubles as warm-up activation. Useful most sessions.

D · TRUNK & POSTURAL ENDURANCE

Trunk

A 24-hour race is a posture-endurance event. These build a trunk that holds you upright and stable when everything's tired — anti-movement > crunches.

D1Suitcase carry (loaded carry)

Trunk

Anti-lateral-flexion under load while walking — almost perfect transfer to staying tall and stable under a pack for hours. Underrated for ultras.

Dose
3 × ~30m / side
Cue
Ribs down, hips level
Progress
Heavier / longer
Execution
Heavy DB/KB in one hand, walk tall and level, resisting the lean toward the weight. Switch sides.

D2Pallof press

Trunk

Anti-rotation — trains the trunk to resist the twist that creeps in with fatigue and feeds compensatory chains. Good carryover to the left-side rotation tendency.

Dose
3 × 10 / side
Cue
Slow, no twist
Progress
Stronger band / longer hold
Execution
Band/cable at chest height to the side. Press the handle straight out and resist being rotated, hold, return. Stay square.

D3Side plank (+ variations)

TrunkLateral chain

Lateral trunk + glute medius endurance in one. Holds the pelvis stable late in the race when the left side wants to give.

Dose
3 × :30–:45 / side
Progress
Longer hold / leg raise
Execution
Forearm under shoulder, body in a line, hips up. Build from knees → full → top-leg raises. Hold steady, breathe.

D4Dead bug & bird dog

Trunk

Trunk control with limb movement and breathing — fits naturally with Effer's breathing-led approach. Teaches a stable core while arms and legs work, which is what running actually is.

Dose
2–3 × 8 / side
Cue
Slow + exhale on reach
Progress
Slower / longer levers
Dead bug
On your back, low back gently pressed down, slowly lower opposite arm + leg, exhale, return.
Bird dog
On hands and knees, extend opposite arm + leg without tipping, hold, switch.

E · CALF & LOWER LEG

Effer-led zone

WHOLE SECTION GOVERNED BY EFFER

The calf is the limiter, and the working diagnosis (plantarflexor hyperexcitability / EAMC on the altered-neuromuscular-control model) means more calf load is not automatically better. Treat this section as a checklist of what calf work exists, not licence to program it yourself. Volume, progression and whether to load at all are Effer's calls. Build strength without tipping into the over-firing pattern — and respect the flagged tendency to over-test.

E1Seated calf raise (soleus)

Calf · check Effer

Soleus is the endurance calf muscle and a big player in distance running and descending control. Bent-knee position biases it.

Execution
Seated, knees bent ~90°, weight on thighs, raise through the balls of the feet, full controlled lower. Heel-elevation and big-toe cues per Effer.
Dose
Only at the dose Effer sets.

E2Standing calf raise (gastrocnemius)

Calf · check Effer

Straight-knee calf raise biases the gastroc — the power calf for push-off. Single-leg builds the side-to-side balance running demands.

Execution
Standing, knees straight, rise high, lower slowly with control. Progress bilateral → single-leg only when cleared.
Dose
Only at the dose Effer sets. Stop at any sign of the cramp/over-firing pattern.

FITTING IT TO THE 23-WEEK BUILD

One strength session a week, mapped to the phases of the run/hike/bike/row plan. The job of strength shifts from building to maintaining as the mountain volume takes over.

PHASE WEEKS TEMPLATES STRENGTH JOB
BaseW1–8 (Jun–Jul)T1 / T2 / T3 rotate Build the durability bank. Heaviest loads, full sessions, progress weekly. Running's restricted, so strength is high-value now.
BuildW9–16 (Aug–Sep)T1 / T2 / T3, drop to T4 on big weeks Maintain strength as hill/vert volume climbs. If a Peak B2B week is huge, use T4 so strength doesn't add fatigue.
PeakW17–19 (Oct)T4, then T5 Pull volume right back. Maintenance only — protect the big mountain days.
TaperW20–23 (Oct–Nov)T5 Keep the patterns alive, shed all fatigue. Last session ~7–10 days out.

PROGRESSION RULES — BASE & BUILD

  • Add reps before load. Hit the top of the rep range with 2–3 RIR for two sessions, then nudge weight up and drop back to the bottom of the range.
  • Slow the tempo as a progression in its own right — a 5-second eccentric is harder and more descent-specific than just more weight.
  • Left side leads the dose. On single-leg work, the weaker/affected side sets the reps and load; the strong side matches, not exceeds.
  • One variable at a time. Don't add load, reps and range in the same week.

WHEN TO BACK OFF — NO HEROICS

  • Any hint of the calf cramp / over-firing pattern → stop calf work, tell Effer.
  • Strength soreness bleeding into the Friday Peak day → cut volume or move the session earlier in the week.
  • Left knee/hip control degrading mid-set → end the set; it's a quality drill, not an ego one.
  • Big mountain B2B coming → downgrade to T4/T5 that week. The race is built on the hills, not the gym.

THE ONE-LINE SUMMARY

Strength here is insurance for the descents and the left chain, and an engine for the climbs — a supporting act to the hills, bike, row and Effer's rehab. Keep it consistent, keep it light enough to never cost a Peak day, and taper it out so you arrive in Finestrat fresh.
